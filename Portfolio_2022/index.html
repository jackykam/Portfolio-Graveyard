Javascript Game

For a highschool introduction to information technology, our class was tasked with developing an application or game through a means of our choice. A friend and I opted to create a combat based RPG inspired by the whimsical nature of games such as West of Loathing. A majority of the game was designed using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with character stats and inventory saved and loaded via PHP.

This was my first introduction into coding, so many challenges arose when working with PHP and JavaScript such as character data transfer and decision trees. We also had to learn about the use of objects in order to create a flexible and scalable combat system without hard-coding a multitude of cases or if-statements. The use of object properties allowed for easy indexing and avoided any confusion of multi-dimensional arrays, all while remaining easy to parse.

By the end of the term, I developed an understanding of the coding fundamentals, and was introduced to object-oriented programming. The result was a turn-based combat RPG with class selection, a currency/market system, and of course, silly dialogue. Not much a game to take itself too seriously, the creative freedom involved in designing such a project helped spark my passion for game design and scripting.

Metalwork Shield

Metalwork has always been a huge passion of mine, and served as an excellent medium to express creative freedom. This has allowed me to learn the various shop machines as well as proper design methods and welding. This project was a design piece for my senior year in our high-school automotive shop. A friend and I opted to create a wearable replica of the Wakandan Shield given to Captain America in Avengers: Infinity War.

We created stencils first from paper for shape, then cardboard for fit and feel, before transferring over to 20-gauge sheet metal. Due to the thickness of the metal, we were unable to directly spot weld the sheets, which lead to the opportunity to use a variety of sheet metal tools. Crimpers helped create tapered curvature, while flanging and punches allowed us to get parallel sheets flush enough to MIG weld. This project really helped establish many of the first steps into manufacturing, providing skills in welding and the various shop tools alongside the design process.

CAD Keychain

Our introduction to Computer Aided Design was a competition among first year engineering students to design a small keychain. I designed a rectangular fidget toy comprised of two clamshell halves which would house rotatable gears. The separate parts were modelled in the OnShape CAD program.

This was my first experience designing a printable part in a proper CAD program, and required that I learn the conventions of proper design etiquette. Designing for manufacturing meant thinking about the orientation each component could be printed in, as well as how they would fit together to allow for movement. Furthermore, understanding of the material from which the part would be printed determined the thicknesses and allowable tolerances for each part.

Line-Following Robot

This introductory second-year project combined microcontrollers and basic robotics to create a car which could track a black line on a sheet of paper. IR sensors mounted on the underside of the robot detect the line below, and an Arduino Uno processes the sensor data before deciding how to proceed. The robot is capable of traversing sharp turns, crossroads, and even small gaps in the path based on contextual inference. Much optimization went into the code to minimize the computation needed for a given logic cycle, which would reduce the overall time required to complete a given course.

Alongside the controls engineering aspect, this was an engaging design introduction to the multidisciplinary nature of the Integrated Engineering program, combining software, electronics, and some product design. The finalized robot was able to complete 95% of the given courses, with a completion time of just under 60 seconds.

Audio Synth Glove

A second year capstone project, my team and I wanted to place a unique spin on modern audio synthesizers by designing a glove to translate user hand gestures into music. An ESP32 microcontroller communicated sensor data over WiFi to a user interface, using the user's existing IP address to host a webserver. I was the software lead for the team, tasked with creating a wiring schematic of the microcontroller, obtaining sensor values and relaying them from the microcontroller to the computer, before displaying the information through a UI. The UI was designed using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with the microcontroller coded in the Arduino IDE.

This being my first time working with wireless communication capable microcontrollers, I hit a major roadblock when attempting to communicate with the computer. Initial plans were to use Bluetooth BLE, which was later scrapped due to its shorter effective range and latency issues. Furthermore, we encountered several issues with sensor calibration which led to drifting sensor output and inaccuracies. This was eventually addressed by constraining the sensors using 3D printed enclosures, and implementing threshold values to help filter out extraneous noise.

Our final product incorporated flex sensors to adjust the audio pitch, tone, and volume values, while incorporating basic gestures such as swipe and push to jump between tracks. This was a multidisciplinary project combining 3D modelling with electronics and software to create a cohesive and working prototype.

Our project went on to win first place in the Second-Year IGEN Design Award, which is determined by the voting of the second, third, and fourth-year IGEN students (Monetary value $150 per person, $900 total).

Tremor Stabilization Glove

Working alongside 5 interdisciplinary students, our third year project was a wearable device capable of dampening Essential Tremor in the hands. We used a combination of accelerometer, gyrometer, and electromyography (EMG) sensors to record user hand motion. A motorized flywheel induces gyroscopic stabilization to counteract tremor amplitude, while a machine learning algorithm discerns between a hand tremor and voluntary motion. I took on the role of team lead, monitoring all aspects of the project from budget, to mechanical design, and electrical integration.

Due to the oscillatory nature of Essential Tremor, careful thought and consideration was required to choose a motor with a high rpm and torque, while maintaining low profile and power draw. As for data collection, our initial algorithm had difficulties correlating sensor data with the occurrence of tremor (accuracy ~60%). To achieve higher accuracy, we tested dozens of neural net models, tinkering with loss functions and number of layers to achieve an accuracy of ~90%.

There is still much to learn in applying machine learning, which is partially limited to the quantity and quality of available sample data. Regardless, this was an excellent opportunity for me to apply my leadership, hands-on, and software skills into a project which produced a applicable final result.

Our project went on to win first place in the Third-Year IGEN Design Award, which is determined by the voting of the general public, as well as second, third, and fourth-year IGEN students (Monetary value $175 per person, $1050 total).

Drone Claw Assembly

As a mechanical designer and integration specialist for the UBC UAS design team, I aided in the design, fabrication, and implementation of a claw system for retrieving packages. This system utilized a pair of linear actuators to clamp the payload, as well as a swiveling plate to cradle the object once it was lifted off the ground. Simultaneously, I worked with another member of the team manufacturing and assembling a set of landing gear for the drone, which would allow it to safely land and maneuver up to the package.

We faced a multitude of issues during the integration process, as the coordination of many moving parts would prove difficult, which was compounded by a weight budget requiring further simplification. This was addressed by using waterjet cut aluminum plates to form the landing gear, which reduced on weight while remaining stable, and redesigning the claw mechanism to not require additional electronics to cradle the object.

This project was an incredible opportunity to expand my knowledge about mechatronics; enhancing my knowledge in SolidWorks, as well as working with more complex manufacturing methods including the waterjet machine and laser cutter. Despite a tight timeline, I was able to apply my skills and help reduce manufacturing and assembly times of over 80 aluminum and sheet metal parts.

A Brief History

Since my youth, i've been inquisitive by nature (much to the dismay of my parents), taking apart electronics and other gadgets to see how the parts interact and how they could be applied in a different context. I found the world of mechanical design, physical modelling, and software to be fascinating, and hopped on any opportunity to learn just a little more every day.

High school was where I hit my stride, being exposed to a plethora of technical skills and experiences. Through our info tech courses I was introduced to web design with HTML and JavaScript, while theatre production allowed me to experiment with carpentry alongside audio and lighting tech. Lastly our automotive shop was where I would learn the ropes of metalwork, welding, and manufacturing tools, alongside vehicle mechanics. Each opportunity more captivating than the last, this was what truly cemented my decision to pursue a degree in engineering.

Moving onto university saw a transition from shop tools and writing simple code in a notepad file to CNC, 3D printing, CAD and microcontrollers, yet the wonder and amusement of creating something from scratch remains the same. Through student design teams and project courses, I have been able to expand on the various skills built throughout the years, and test the knowledge I had gained in unique and applicable ways. The satisfaction of a completed project is almost an addiction, and I will continue to pursue more challenging ventures in the future.
